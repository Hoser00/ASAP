Water supply

Water…what a mundane, boring subject. Or is it? Most people don't give water much more than a passing thought. And that thought most likely will just be why they want some water. You know, “I'm thirsty”, or “I need to wash the dishes”, or “My potted plant is wilting and it needs a drink of water”. I think you get the idea. For most people it is just a matter of twisting the knob and 'wha la', there's water, hot or cold, as much as you want whenever you want. However, that thought process (or lack there of) is becoming increasingly understood as not a realistic or sustainable expectation. There is a huge infrastructure requiring a tremendous amount of energy that is out there that is required to allow you that incredible luxury of just turning on a tap and having safe, clean abundant water right at your finger tips any time of the day or night.

Some of us are finally becoming aware that water isn't an inexhaustible resource at our disposal. In fact, we are becoming more and more aware of water shortages and severe limitations on clean safe drinking water. Water rights have always been a source of conflict, manipulation and control, but these rights are now becoming huge monetary expenses that are involving bitter political maneuvering including the passage of onerous laws and regulations and hair brain diversion schemes. In today's environment, water is anything but a boring or mundane subject! Without water, life as we know it ends abruptly.

Water issues

  • Understanding water source and distribution methods in the Sierras and the foothills….getting our 'source' water rights back

  • A brief discussion on the extensive treatment process to deliver safe drinking water and the onerous State regulations and expensive testing processes that are foisted upon water agencies such as the Tuolumne Utilites District

  • Alternative sources of water supply to supplement our fresh water supply. ie. Grey water, roof rain water recapture, minimizing hardscaping around structures, yard sculpting to hold water to help recharge ground water supplies, raw water delivery for yard and garden uses

  • True useful water conservation and the use of water to best advantage without doing 'without'

  • Learning and implementing water conserving plumbing systems in not only new homes and structures, but remodels and renovations of existing structures

  • Embracing yard landscaping that leans towards locally natural growing plants and trees

  • Proper irrigation to 'get the job done' but not ruin our soil because of saline, etc.

  • Becoming pro-active and helping your local water agencies do the proper and right job of providing the 'right' kind of water at a reasonable cost

  • An action list of possible things, both big and small, that a typical homeowner may want to study and possibly implement on their own property that will conserve water and monies spent on water related items
